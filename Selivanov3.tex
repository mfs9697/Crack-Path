\begin{abstract}
	We present a compact finite-element framework for incipient mixed-mode crack
	kinking in plane strain, in which the physical crack is augmented by a short,
	zero-thickness cohesive continuation. A thin auxiliary channel is used only
	for mesh generation and is collapsed after T6 conversion, so that the
	analysis is carried out on the physical interface with coincident node pairs.
	The cohesive law is potential-based, differentiable, and bounded in both the
	normal and tangential directions; the corresponding cohesive potential yields
	an energetically consistent formulation with a symmetric Hessian tangent and
	robust Newton convergence.
	
	The construction of the interface degrees of freedom, jump operator,
	line-integration operators, and consistent linearization is detailed
	explicitly. Two complementary selectors are considered for the kink
	direction: a global load-based criterion and a local energetic mouth
	criterion. Numerical studies show rapid convergence of the critical load,
	stable determination of the kink angle, and close agreement between bulk
	tractions and cohesive tractions reconstructed from the displacement jumps
	along almost the entire cohesive leg.
	
	The framework is examined deliberately in a near-LEFM regime. In this
	setting, the predicted kink direction remains close to the classical elastic
	benchmark, whereas the critical load exhibits a clearer sensitivity to the
	finite cohesive zone and to the traction--separation law. A calibrated
	sensitivity study of the auxiliary cohesive-leg length shows that, when the
	active cohesive zone is kept well contained within the trial segment, the
	directional prediction depends only weakly on this auxiliary choice. The method is thus best suited to quasi-static mixed-mode crack kinking in a near-LEFM regime, where it provides a controlled finite-process-zone counterpart to classical elastic predictors.
	\keywords{Mixed-mode fracture \and Cohesive zone model \and
		Traction--separation law \and Potential-based cohesive interface}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Accurate prediction of how and where cracks deflect is essential in many
engineering systems, from layered composites and adhesive joints to
microelectronic packages and structural components subjected to non-uniform
loading. Even small deviations in crack trajectory can alter load-carrying
capacity, trigger delamination, or precipitate catastrophic failure.
Classical Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM) provides canonical
predictions of the incipient kink direction, notably via the maximum
circumferential-stress criterion and related energy-based arguments
\cite{ErdoganSih1963}. Path conditions such as enforcing
$K_{\rm II}=0$ at an advancing crack tip \cite{CotterellRice1980}
provide equivalent selection principles. For cracks interacting with
material interfaces, early analyses established when a crack continues
along the interface or kinks into an adjacent medium, highlighting the
dominant role of the mixed-mode elastic field in steering the initial
deflection \cite{HeHutchinson1989}.

Cohesive-zone formulations complement LEFM by embedding fracture processes
directly in traction--separation relations (TSLs), thereby linking intact
material response with complete decohesion without explicitly tracking the
crack. Foundational models idealised the process zone using simple cohesive
laws \cite{Barenblatt1962,Dugdale1960}, and subsequent finite-element
implementations showed that such laws can robustly capture initiation,
deflection, branching, and arrest in brittle and quasi-brittle materials
\cite{CamachoOrtiz1996}. Cohesive laws have also been central to
interpreting and predicting resistance curves
\cite{TvergaardHutchinson1992} and to modelling dynamic crack growth with
intrinsic interface behaviour \cite{XuNeedleman1994}.

\paragraph{Mixed-mode fracture and cohesive laws.}
A comprehensive review by Park and Paulino \cite{Park2011Review}
distinguishes two broad families of mixed-mode models:
\emph{energy-based} criteria and \emph{traction-based} cohesive laws.
Energy-based schemes---such as the widely used Benzeggagh--Kenane (BK)
criterion \cite{BenzeggaghKenane1996}---prescribe a mode-dependent
fracture toughness but do not supply interface tractions and cannot be
derived from a single potential. Traction-based cohesive laws, initiated
by Xu and Needleman \cite{XuNeedleman1994}, define an explicit mixed-mode
potential $\Psi(d_n,d_t)$ whose derivatives give the physical tractions.
Subsequent extensions introduced polynomial, power-law, exponential, and
asymmetric couplings, allowing the mode transition and shear sensitivity
to be tuned to the material system. Such potential-based cohesive laws
ensure thermodynamic consistency and yield symmetric algorithmic tangents,
which is advantageous for implicit finite-element formulations and
particularly relevant in mixed-mode initiation and kinking problems.

The importance of accurately describing the near-tip fracture-process zone
(FPZ) has motivated several recent modelling developments. Najmeddine,
Gupta and Moini \cite{NajmeddineGuptaMoini2025} coupled a phase-field
description of bulk fracture with a PPR cohesive interface to capture
deflection, penetration and toughening mechanisms in multi-material
systems, while Shabrou and Daneshjoo \cite{ShabrouDaneshjoo2025} proposed
mixed-mode criteria for orthotropic materials that incorporate FPZ effects
and T-stress, improving directional predictions over classical LEFM
criteria. Analytical progress has likewise sharpened the understanding of
mixed-mode fields: Liu and Wei derived a closed-form stress field for
finitely kinked cracks \cite{Liu2021KinkedStressFields} and later a
semi-analytical stress-intensity-factor framework for branched cracks
\cite{Liu2023BranchedSIF}, Si and Wei extended these ideas to kinked edge
cracks in a half-space \cite{Si2024KinkedEdge}, and Shi and Xu obtained
explicit stress-intensity factors for infinitesimal kinks in orthotropic
solids \cite{Shi2025OrthotropicKinkSIF}. Parallel cohesive-zone studies
have clarified how mode mixity, cohesive strength and interface toughness
compete to determine crack deflection versus penetration
\cite{ParmigianiThouless2006CZMDeflection,Alam2016IncidentAngleCZM,
	Tankasala2017KinkingOrthotropic,ChenDeBorst2022PFRCZM}.

\paragraph{Motivating example: why cohesive models remain relevant even in a near-LEFM regime.}
Even in configurations where LEFM is nominally applicable, cohesive-zone
effects can produce measurable changes in the predicted critical load. For
a mode-I crack of half-length $a$ in an infinite plate, LEFM predicts
onset of fracture when $K_{\mathrm{I}}=\sigma\sqrt{\pi a}$ reaches the
toughness $K_{\mathrm{Ic}}$, giving
$\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}^{\mathrm{LEFM}} = K_{\mathrm{Ic}}/\sqrt{\pi a}$.
A cohesive-zone representation such as the Dugdale model replaces the
singular elastic field by a finite process zone of length
$\ell_{\mathrm{p}}$ and consequently yields a slightly smaller critical
load. Even when the process-zone size is only about $5\%$ of the crack
half-length---a regime often treated in practice as close to the LEFM
limit---the cohesive prediction remains $2$--$3\%$ lower than the LEFM
estimate. This modest but systematic deviation shows that a finite process
zone influences not only the near-tip fields but also global quantities
such as the critical load.

This example motivates the viewpoint adopted in the present work. The
question is not whether LEFM should be replaced in the small-process-zone
regime, but how a regularized cohesive formulation can be used to assess,
in a controlled way, which parts of the kinking response remain essentially
elastic and which retain a measurable imprint of the traction--separation
law and of the finite FPZ. In particular, the present study is conducted
deliberately in a near-LEFM regime, so that agreement and discrepancy with
classical predictors can both be interpreted meaningfully.

\paragraph{Aim and modelling philosophy.}
Our aim is to isolate, in the cleanest possible setting, how mode mixity,
cohesive strength, and crack orientation conspire to determine the kink
direction under mixed-mode loading, and how this choice manifests in the
critical load. To this end, we employ a deliberately minimal plane-strain
finite-element formulation in which the physical crack is extended by a
short, zero-thickness cohesive continuation. A slender auxiliary channel
is introduced solely to control mesh resolution and is collapsed prior to
loading, ensuring that all tractions and boundary conditions apply
directly to the true crack faces. For each trial orientation, we compute a
critical state dictated solely by the prescribed traction--separation law
and examine two complementary selectors for the kink direction: a global
load-based criterion and a local cohesive-energetic criterion at the
mouth.

A second objective is to distinguish clearly between constitutive effects
and auxiliary numerical choices. To this end, the analysis examines how the
predicted direction depends on the finite trial cohesive length used to
regularize the near-tip field, and identifies the range of applicability
and the potential failure modes of the method. This point is important
because the present framework does not evaluate a tip criterion at a
mathematical crack tip; instead, it infers the preferred direction from a
short cohesive continuation of finite extent. The method should therefore
be interpreted as a finite-process-zone formulation intended for controlled
comparison with LEFM in a small but nonzero process-zone regime.

This modelling philosophy avoids remeshing, enrichment, or
geometry-specific heuristics and reveals, with high transparency, which
aspects of the cohesive law influence the kink direction and which enter
primarily through the evolution of the fracture-process zone and the
critical load. At the same time, it provides a natural setting for
examining numerical robustness, including the sensitivity to the auxiliary
cohesive-leg length and the conditioning of different directional
selectors.

\section{Problem statement}
\label{sec:problem}

We consider a two-dimensional body in plane strain containing a predefined
two-leg crack path that serves as the basis for studying mixed-mode crack
kinking under quasi-static loading conditions. The problem setup, geometry,
local frames, and representative traction--separation law are summarized in
Fig.~\ref{fig:statement}.

The body, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:stmt:a}, is a rectangular domain subjected
to uniform tensile tractions on its top and bottom edges, while the vertical
sides remain free of external loads. The internal discontinuity consists of
two consecutive legs forming a polyline. The first leg (Leg~1) represents the
existing physical crack of prescribed length and orientation, whereas the
second leg (Leg~2) is a short fictitious continuation introduced to realize a
cohesive interface on which kinking may occur. We denote by
$\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}$ the boundary of the physical crack (Leg~1), and by
$\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}$ the boundary of the cohesive extension (Leg~2). The
kink originates at the common junction point of the two legs, referred to
here as the knee, and terminates at the trial cohesive tip. The orientation
of Leg~2 relative to Leg~1 defines the kink angle. The material is assumed
homogeneous and isotropic, and plane-strain conditions are imposed
throughout.

A closer view of the two-leg configuration is presented in
Fig.~\ref{fig:stmt:b}. The physical crack, Leg~1, is traction-free, whereas
Leg~2 carries cohesive tractions acting on its two opposing faces. These
tractions depend on the local relative displacements between the upper and
lower faces and are expressed in a local coordinate system rigidly attached
to Leg~2. The local frame consists of a tangential direction aligned with the
cohesive segment and a normal direction pointing from the lower to the upper
face. The corresponding tractions therefore form a pair of equal and opposite
vectors acting on the two sides of the cohesive zone.

The distribution of openings and tractions along the cohesive continuation is
sketched in Fig.~\ref{fig:stmt:c}. Along Leg~2, the normal opening and the
tangential sliding evolve according to the adopted traction--separation law.
The active cohesive region is bounded by the physical crack tip and the trial
cohesive tip, whereas the remaining crack faces are traction-free. The local
coordinates $(s,r)$ are used to describe variations of the opening and the
traction along and across the interface.

\paragraph{Choice of the auxiliary cohesive-leg length \texorpdfstring{$\delta$}{delta}.}
The cohesive-leg length $\delta$ defines the available extent over which the
finite fracture-process zone may develop along the trial continuation. Within
the present formulation, $\delta$ is not a material parameter but an
auxiliary numerical length introduced to regularize the singular elastic
near-tip field by replacing it with a finite cohesive zone. For this
regularization to be meaningful, $\delta$ must be sufficiently long to
contain the full active cohesive zone. Choosing $\delta$ too short
artificially truncates the process zone and may leave a residual stress
concentration at the cohesive tip. Once the active cohesive part remains well
contained within the trial segment, however, $\delta$ no longer plays a
constitutive role and the predicted response becomes insensitive to its
precise value.

For trapezoidal cohesive laws, including the piecewise-smooth law adopted in
this work, a practical estimate of the required scale can be obtained from
the Dugdale model, for which the process-zone length $\ell_p$ scales with the
crack size $a$ as
\[
\ell_p = 2\pi a
\left(\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}^{\mathrm{LEFM}}}{\sigma_n^{\max}}\right)^2.
\]
This estimate is used here as a practical guideline for selecting the
auxiliary cohesive length. In the baseline benchmark calculations reported in
Sections~\ref{sec:results} and~\ref{sec:LEFM-comparison}, a representative
fixed value $\delta/a=0.06$ is employed. In the sensitivity study of
Section~\ref{sec:scope_limitations}, by contrast, $\delta$ is chosen
adaptively from a calibrated LEFM-based predictor so that the active-zone
fraction $\beta/\delta$ remains controlled as the material parameters vary.
The role of $\delta$ should therefore be understood throughout the paper as
that of an auxiliary regularization length, not as an intrinsic fracture
parameter.

Figure~\ref{fig:stmt:d} shows the mode-wise traction--separation laws used in
the cohesive formulation. In each pure mode, the traction increases from zero
to the cohesive strength and then decays smoothly to zero at complete
decohesion. The initial rising branch is not intended to represent material
hardening. Its purpose is to regularize the transition from the traction-free
crack faces to the active cohesive region. Without such a strengthening
branch, imposing a constant plateau traction immediately from $d_n=0$ would
create a jump in stress where the cohesive zone meets the traction-free part
of the crack, leading to an unphysical singularity at the process-zone tip.
The rising branch therefore ensures that the cohesive traction approaches its
maximum value smoothly, provides a finite-stress closure of the cohesive
zone, and allows the active cohesive length to emerge as part of the
solution rather than being prescribed in advance.

The objective of the analysis is to determine the preferred direction of
incipient crack advance, or equivalently the kink angle, by evaluating

cohesive law quantitatively, and describe the finite-element construction
used to compute the corresponding critical states.


\section{Governing equations}
\label{sec:governing}

The displacement field
\[
\uu:\Omega\setminus(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}\cup\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}})
\to\mathbb{R}^2
\]
is the primary unknown of the boundary-value problem. The body is treated as
a homogeneous, isotropic, linear-elastic solid in plane strain, and
equilibrium is assumed under quasi-static loading conditions.

\paragraph{Bulk kinematics and constitutive law.}
The infinitesimal strain tensor and the Cauchy stress tensor are related by
the linear-elastic law
\begin{equation}
	\eps(\uu)=\tfrac12(\grad\uu+\grad\uu^\top),
	\qquad
	\sig=\four D:\eps(\uu),
	\label{eq:constitutive}
\end{equation}
where \(\four D\) is the fourth-order isotropic elasticity tensor defined by
the Young's modulus \(E\) and Poisson's ratio \(\nu\). Under plane-strain
conditions, the out-of-plane stress satisfies
\[
\sigma_{zz}=\nu(\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}),
\]
and the shear modulus is \(G=E/[2(1+\nu)]\).

\paragraph{Equilibrium in the bulk.}
In the absence of body forces, the stress field in the bulk satisfies
\begin{equation}
	\diver\sig=\vb 0
	\qquad
	\text{in }
	\Omega\setminus(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}\cup\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}),
	\label{eq:equilibrium}
\end{equation}
where \(\diver(\cdot)\) denotes the row-wise divergence of a second-order
tensor. Thus, the elastic field is governed by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:equilibrium} everywhere in the body except along the physical
crack and the cohesive continuation.

\paragraph{Boundary and interface conditions.}
Let \(\vb n_b\) denote the outward unit normal to \(\partial\Omega\). Uniform
tractions are applied on the horizontal edges,
\[
\sig\,\vb n_b=
\begin{cases}
	+\sigma\,\ey, & y=+B,\\[2pt]
	-\sigma\,\ey, & y=-B,
\end{cases}
\qquad
\sig\,\vb n_b=\vb 0
\quad\text{on }x=0\text{ and }x=A,
\]
where the scalar \(\sigma\) is the prescribed far-field load factor.

To suppress rigid-body motion, we impose the minimal point constraints
\[
\uu(A,0)\cdot\ey=0,
\qquad
\uu(0,B)\cdot\ex=0,
\qquad
\uu(0,-B)\cdot\ex=0,
\]
which fix one vertical and two horizontal degrees of freedom without
otherwise restricting the solution.

Across the faces of the physical crack \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}\), the
tractions vanish:
\[
\sig\,\bn=\vb 0
\qquad
\text{on }\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}^\pm .
\]
Along the cohesive continuation \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}\), the displacement
jump and cohesive traction are related through
\begin{equation}
	\bd=\QQ\,\jump{\uu},
	\qquad
	\bTcz=\bTcz(\bd)\in\mathbb{R}^2,
	\label{eq:cohesive_relations}
\end{equation}
where \(\QQ=[\bt^\top;\bn^\top]\) is the local orthonormal frame attached to
Leg~2, \(\bd=(d_t,d_n)\) are the local tangential and normal components of
the displacement jump, and \(\bTcz=(\sigma_t,\sigma_n)\) is the
corresponding cohesive traction vector. The cohesive response is taken as a
nonlinear, history-independent function of the jump, and traction continuity
across the interface implies
\[
\bTcz^{(+)}=-\,\bTcz^{(-)} .
\]
The specific functional form of \(\bTcz(\bd)\) is given by the
potential-based mixed-mode traction--separation law introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:tsl}.

\paragraph{Weak form.}
Let \(\Gamma_N\subset\partial\Omega\) denote the traction-loaded part of the
external boundary. Multiplying Eq.~\eqref{eq:equilibrium} by an admissible
virtual displacement field \(\delta\uu\) and integrating by parts gives the
variational form
\begin{equation}
	\int_{\Omega\setminus(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}\cup\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}})}
	\eps(\delta\uu):\sig\,\dd\Omega
	-
	\int_{\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}}
	\delta\bd^\top\,\bTcz\,\dd s
	=
	\int_{\Gamma_N}
	\delta\uu^\top(\sig\,\vb n_b)\,\dd s .
	\label{eq:weak_form}
\end{equation}
The first integral is the bulk elastic contribution, the second represents
the work of cohesive tractions over the displacement jumps along
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}\), and the right-hand side is the external work of the
applied boundary tractions.

Equation~\eqref{eq:weak_form} provides the starting point for the finite-element
discretization developed in Section~\ref{sec:construction}. In this form, the
cohesive contribution enters only through the surface integral over
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}\), which makes it possible to linearize the coupled
bulk-interface problem consistently while preserving a smooth transition from
the intact state to complete separation.


\section{Cohesive interface and traction--separation law}
\label{sec:tsl}

The cohesive behavior along \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}\) is governed by a
potential-based traction--separation law. Local quantities are expressed in
the Leg--2 frame
\[
\mathbf{Q}=
\begin{bmatrix}
	\mathbf{t}^{\top}\\
	\mathbf{n}^{\top}
\end{bmatrix},
\]
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:problem}. The face-to-face displacement jump
resolves as
\[
\mathbf{d}=(d_t,d_n)^{\top}=\mathbf{Q}\jump{\mathbf{u}},
\]
with normalized openings
\[
\bar d_t=\frac{|d_t|}{d_t^{\max}},
\qquad
\bar d_n=\frac{d_n}{d_n^{\max}}.
\]
The cohesive strengths are denoted by \(\sigma_t^{\max}\) and
\(\sigma_n^{\max}\). Together with the mode-I and mode-II fracture energies
\(\phi_{\alpha}\) (\(\alpha\in\{n,t\}\)), these strengths constitute the
independent fracture parameters of the model.

The critical openings \(d_{\alpha}^{\max}\) are introduced as characteristic
length scales and are determined from the fracture-energy condition
\begin{equation}
	\phi_{\alpha}
	=
	\sigma_{\alpha}^{\max} d_{\alpha}^{\max} c_{\alpha},
	\qquad
	c_{\alpha}=F_{\alpha}(1),
	\qquad
	F_{\alpha}(\xi)=\int_{0}^{\xi}\tau_{\alpha}(s)\,\mathrm{d}s.
	\label{eq:phi_energy}
\end{equation}
Here \(\tau_{\alpha}(\xi)\) is the normalized one-dimensional
traction--separation law in pure mode \(\alpha\),
\(F_{\alpha}(\xi)\) is the associated dimensionless potential, and
\(c_{\alpha}\) is the corresponding dimensionless area under the normalized
traction curve. It is convenient to introduce the energetic prefactors
\begin{equation}
	\chi_{\alpha}:=\sigma_{\alpha}^{\max}d_{\alpha}^{\max}
	=
	\frac{\phi_{\alpha}}{c_{\alpha}},
	\qquad
	\alpha\in\{n,t\},
	\label{eq:chi_def}
\end{equation}
which set the mode-wise energy scales of the cohesive potential. These
quantities are not additional material parameters; they follow directly from
\((\sigma_{\alpha}^{\max},\phi_{\alpha})\) and the chosen shape of the
traction--separation law.

For notational brevity, once the pure-mode laws have been specified, we write
\(F(\cdot)\), \(\tau(\cdot)\), and \(g(\cdot)=\tau'(\cdot)\) for the
corresponding mode-dependent functions, with the active mode understood from
the argument and the associated parameter set.

\paragraph{Mode-wise (one-dimensional) law.}
The physical role of the strengthening branch has already been discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:problem}; here we specify the particular smooth law used in
the computations. In each pure mode, the normalized traction consists of a
strengthening branch, a plateau, and a softening branch. The parameters
\(0<a_1<a_2<1\) define the end of the strengthening interval and the onset of
softening, respectively. In applications, these parameters may be chosen
separately for the normal and tangential modes; the mode index is omitted here
for compactness.

The shape of the one-dimensional traction--separation law is not merely a
numerical convenience. As emphasized by Shet and Chandra \cite{ShetChandra2003}, the detailed form of the traction--separation curve influences how the fracture-process zone
develops and therefore affects the fracture response beyond the sole values of
the peak traction and the total dissipated energy. This is the reason for
retaining explicit control over the strengthening interval, plateau, and
softening branch in the present formulation. 

For the normal mode, compression is regularized by a linear penalty branch.
The normalized traction \(\tau\), its derivative \(g=\tau'\), and the
associated potential \(F\) are written in the compact form

\begin{multline}
	\label{eq:tau_law}
		(F,\tau,g)(\xi)= \\
		\begin{cases}
			\big(\tfrac{\kappa}{2}\,\xi^2,\ \kappa\,\xi,\ \kappa\big),
			& \xi\le 0,\\[2pt]
			\big(\tfrac{\xi^2}{a_1}\bigl(1-\tfrac{\xi}{3a_1}\bigr),\
			\tfrac{\xi}{a_1}\bigl(2-\tfrac{\xi}{a_1}\bigr),\
			\tfrac{2}{a_1}\bigl(1-\tfrac{\xi}{a_1}\bigr)\big),
			& 0<\xi<a_1,\\[4pt]
			(\xi-\tfrac{a_1}{3},\ 1,\ 0),
			& a_1\le \xi<a_2,\\[4pt]
			\Big(
			c-\!\!\int_{\xi}^{1}\! \tau(s)\,\mathrm ds,\\
			\qquad\frac{(1+2\xi-3a_2)(1-\xi)^2}{(1-a_2)^3},\
			-\frac{6(1-\xi)(\xi-a_2)}{(1-a_2)^3}
			\Big),
			& a_2\le\xi\le 1,\\[8pt]
			(c,\ 0,\ 0),
			& \xi>1.
		\end{cases}
\end{multline}
Here
\begin{equation}
	c=\frac{1}{6}\left(3-2a_1+3a_2\right)
	\label{eq:c_def}
\end{equation}
is the dimensionless area under the normalized traction--separation law.
Choosing
\[
\kappa=\frac{2}{a_1}
\]
gives
\[
\tau(0^-)=\tau(0^+)=0,
\qquad
g(0^-)=g(0^+)=\frac{2}{a_1},
\]
so the contact--opening transition is \(C^1\)-smooth.

The dimensional pure-mode laws are expressed in terms of the normalized
openings \(\bar d_n\) and \(\bar d_t\). For the normal mode we use
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_n(d_n)=
	\begin{cases}
		\sigma_n^{\max}\,\tau(\bar d_n), & d_n\ge 0,\\[3pt]
		k_c\,d_n, & d_n<0,
	\end{cases}
	\qquad
	k_c=\kappa\,\frac{\sigma_n^{\max}}{d_n^{\max}}.
	\label{eq:sigma_n_pure}
\end{equation}
For the tangential mode we define the shear magnitude and the signed traction
as
\begin{equation}
	|\sigma_t|(d_t)=\sigma_t^{\max}\,\tau(\bar d_t),
	\qquad
	\sigma_t(d_t)=|\sigma_t|(d_t)\,\operatorname{sgn}_0(d_t),
	\label{eq:sigma_t_pure}
\end{equation}
where \(\operatorname{sgn}_0(0)=0\).

The corresponding one-dimensional tangents are
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\mathrm{d}\sigma_n}{\mathrm{d}d_n}=
	\begin{cases}
		\dfrac{\sigma_n^{\max}}{d_n^{\max}}\,g(\bar d_n), & d_n>0,\\[8pt]
		k_c, & d_n<0,
	\end{cases}
	\qquad
	\frac{\mathrm{d}|\sigma_t|}{\mathrm{d}|d_t|}
	=
	\frac{\sigma_t^{\max}}{d_t^{\max}}\,g(\bar d_t).
	\label{eq:pure_tangents}
\end{equation}
The pure-mode cohesive potentials are then
\begin{equation}
	\Psi_{\alpha}(d_{\alpha})
	=
	\chi_{\alpha}F(\bar d_{\alpha})
	=
	\frac{\phi_{\alpha}}{c_{\alpha}}\,F(\bar d_{\alpha}),
	\qquad
	\alpha\in\{n,t\}.
	\label{eq:pure_potentials}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Potential-based mixed-mode coupling.}
The conservative mixed-mode response is defined through a single scalar
cohesive potential. Starting from the pure-mode energetic contributions
\(\chi_nF(\bar d_n)\) and \(\chi_tF(\bar d_t)\), we introduce the coupled
potential
\begin{equation}
	\Psi(d_n,d_t)
	=
	\chi_nF(\bar d_n)
	+
	\chi_tF(\bar d_t)
	-
	r\sqrt{\chi_n\chi_t}\,F(\bar d_n)F(\bar d_t),
	\label{eq:psi_mixed}
\end{equation}
where \(r\) is a dimensionless coupling parameter. This form is a minimal
energetic extension of the uncoupled law: it is derived from a single
potential, recovers the pure normal or pure tangential response when the
complementary opening vanishes, and preserves variational consistency.

Admissibility requires the multiplicative degradation factors in the
resulting tractions to remain non-negative over the admissible range
\(0\le F(\xi)\le c_{\alpha}\). This yields the bound
\begin{equation}
	0\le r\le r_{\max}
	=
	\min\!\left(
	\frac{1}{c_n}\sqrt{\frac{\chi_n}{\chi_t}},
	\frac{1}{c_t}\sqrt{\frac{\chi_t}{\chi_n}}
	\right).
	\label{eq:rmax}
\end{equation}
Within this range, the coupling weakens the normal resistance when the
tangential opening grows, and conversely weakens the tangential resistance
when the normal opening grows.

The cohesive traction vector follows from the gradient of the potential,
\(\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{cz}}=\partial\Psi/\partial\mathbf{d}
=(\sigma_t,\sigma_n)^{\top}\), which gives
\begin{equation}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_t\\[3pt]
		\sigma_n
	\end{bmatrix}
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_t^{\max}
		\left(
		1-r\sqrt{\dfrac{\chi_n}{\chi_t}}\,F(\bar d_n)
		\right)
		\tau(\bar d_t)\,\operatorname{sgn}_0(d_t)
		\\[12pt]
		\sigma_n^{\max}
		\left(
		1-r\sqrt{\dfrac{\chi_t}{\chi_n}}\,F(\bar d_t)
		\right)
		\tau(\bar d_n)
	\end{bmatrix}.
	\label{eq:Tcz_mixed}
\end{equation}
Thus, the mixed-mode law preserves the one-dimensional traction shape in each
mode but scales it by an energetic degradation factor governed by the opening
in the complementary mode.

\paragraph{Consistent tangent.}
Because \(\Psi\) is a scalar potential, the local algorithmic tangent is the
symmetric Hessian
\[
\mathbf{K}_{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\frac{\partial \mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{cz}}}{\partial \mathbf{d}}.
\]
It is convenient to write it in the factorized form
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{K}_{\mathrm{loc}}
	=
	\mathbf{S}\,\mathbf{M}(\bar d_n,\bar d_t)\,\mathbf{S},
	\qquad
	\mathbf{S}
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_t^{\max}}{d_t^{\max}}} & 0 \\[8pt]
		0 & \sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_n^{\max}}{d_n^{\max}}}
	\end{bmatrix},
	\label{eq:Kloc_factor}
\end{equation}
where the dimensionless symmetric core is
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{M}(\bar d_n,\bar d_t)=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\left(
		1-r\sqrt{\dfrac{\chi_n}{\chi_t}}\,F(\bar d_n)
		\right)g(\bar d_t)
		&
		-r\,\tau(\bar d_t)\tau(\bar d_n)
		\\[10pt]
		-r\,\tau(\bar d_t)\tau(\bar d_n)
		&
		\left(
		1-r\sqrt{\dfrac{\chi_t}{\chi_n}}\,F(\bar d_t)
		\right)g(\bar d_n)
	\end{bmatrix}.
	\label{eq:Mcore}
\end{equation}
This structure is important for the subsequent finite-element formulation.
The tangent is symmetric by construction, which improves the robustness of the
Newton iterations and makes the energetic coupling between the two modes fully
consistent at the discrete level.

\]

\paragraph{Remark on the choice of \texorpdfstring{$a_1$}{a1}.}
The parameter \(a_1\) controls the end of the strengthening branch and
therefore the opening interval over which the cohesive traction rises to its
peak value. In the illustrative plots above, a comparatively large value is
used so that the shape of the law is clearly visible. In the actual kinking
calculations, however, \(a_1\) should be chosen as small as practicable.
Otherwise the apparent opening range of the strengthening branch becomes
artificially extended and the fracture-process zone is correspondingly
over-regularized. In the computations reported later, reducing \(a_1\) did
not produce locking or spurious stiffening under mesh refinement; its main
effect was to increase the number of Newton iterations. The potential-based
formulation is helpful here because the symmetric consistent tangent reduces
the sensitivity of the nonlinear solver to the precise value of \(a_1\).

The present formulation should also be viewed relative to two established
lines of work in cohesive-zone modeling. On the one hand, stabilized
interface-element formulations have been developed to improve robustness when
very stiff cohesive laws are used \cite{Ghosh2019StabilizedCZM,Ghosh2021RobustnessCZM}. On the other hand, potential-based
mixed-mode formulations have been emphasized as a way to preserve
thermodynamic consistency and avoid non-conservative coupling between modes \cite{Dimitri2014ConsistencyCZM}.
The present approach aligns with the latter viewpoint: robustness is sought
primarily through a smooth potential-based law with a symmetric Hessian
tangent, rather than through additional stabilization terms.

\paragraph{Interpretation and limitation of the coupling.}
The potential \(\Psi\) provides a single smooth energetic surface from which
all tractions and tangent moduli follow by differentiation. This guarantees a
conservative interface response, a thermodynamically consistent coupling
between modes, and a symmetric local tangent. These are key advantages for
the implicit finite-element procedure developed in the next section.

At the same time, the potential-based coupling also imposes a clear
limitation. Because both traction components are derived from a single
non-separable energetic function, complete separation in one mode does not,
in general, force the traction in the complementary mode to vanish
independently. As a result, residual complementary tractions may persist in
extreme mixed-mode states. In the parameter range considered here this effect
is small, but it should be kept in mind when interpreting the physical
meaning of the coupled response. The present formulation is therefore best
viewed as a smooth, energetically consistent mixed-mode regularization,
particularly suited to the study of crack initiation and incipient kinking,
rather than as a fully general interface law for arbitrary large-separation
paths.

\section{Cohesive interface discretization and consistent linearization}
\label{sec:construction}

We discretize the cohesive interface \(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}\) by keeping the
two faces of Leg~2 kinematically distinct and pairing coincident node
triplets along the thin channel introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mesh}. The
element operators below are written for a quadratic interface segment \(e\)
with coincident top nodes \((i^+,j^+,k^+)\) and bottom nodes
\((i^-,j^-,k^-)\). The segment is oriented from \(i\) (mouth) to \(k\)
(tip), and a local orthonormal frame
\(\QQ=[\bt^\top;\bn^\top]\) is attached to it tangentially
(mouth\(\to\)tip) and normally (lower\(\to\)upper), as shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:coh-tri}. Within each interface segment, \(\QQ\) is taken
constant and evaluated from the segment chord.

	\centering

	\caption{Quadratic interface segment with coincident node triplets
		\((i^\pm,j^\pm,k^\pm)\). The top edge is \((i^+,j^+,k^+)\); the bottom edge
		is numbered counterclockwise as \((k^-,j^-,i^-)\). The local frame
		\(\QQ=[\bt^\top;\bn^\top]\) is attached to the segment.}
	\label{fig:coh-tri}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Unknown ordering, shape rows, and edge permutation.}
The element displacement vector is assembled by stacking the top-face degrees
of freedom first and the bottom-face degrees of freedom second, with the
bottom ordering reversed so that it follows the counterclockwise edge order
of the lower bulk element:
\begin{align}
	\vb U_e&=
	\big[\ \vb U_e^{+}\ \ \vb U_e^{-}\ \big]^\top
	\in\mathbb{R}^{12},
	\label{eq:seg-Ue}
	\\
	\vb U_e^{+}&=
	\big[
	u_{i^+x}\ u_{i^+y}\ 
	u_{j^+x}\ u_{j^+y}\ 
	u_{k^+x}\ u_{k^+y}
	\big]^\top,
	\nonumber\\
	\vb U_e^{-}&=
	\big[
	u_{k^-x}\ u_{k^-y}\ 
	u_{j^-x}\ u_{j^-y}\ 
	u_{i^-x}\ u_{i^-y}
	\big]^\top.
	\nonumber
\end{align}

Let
\[
N=
\begin{bmatrix}
	N(\xi_i)\\
	N(\xi_j)\\
	N(\xi_k)
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
N(\xi_\ell)=
\big[
N_i(\xi_\ell)\ \ N_j(\xi_\ell)\ \ N_k(\xi_\ell)
\big],
\qquad
\ell\in\{i,j,k\},
\]
denote the stacked scalar quadratic shape rows evaluated at the three
quadratic stations. To account for the reversed bottom-face ordering, we
introduce the node-permutation matrix
\begin{equation}
	\mat P_e=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		0&0&1\\
		0&1&0\\
		1&0&0
	\end{bmatrix},
	\qquad
	(i,j,k)\mapsto(k,j,i).
	\label{eq:seg-Pi}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Explicit jump operator.}
The nodal openings along the interface segment are collected in
\[
\bd_e=
\big[
d_{t,i}\ d_{n,i}\ 
d_{t,j}\ d_{n,j}\ 
d_{t,k}\ d_{n,k}
\big]^\top
\in\mathbb{R}^6.
\]
With the ordering defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:seg-Ue}, these openings are
obtained from the element displacement vector through the jump relation
\begin{equation}
	\bd_e=\mat B_e\,\vb U_e,
	\qquad
	\mat B_e=
	\big[
	\mat B_e^{+}\ \ \mat B_e^{-}
	\big],
	\label{eq:seg-Be}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
	\mat B_e^{+}
	=
	(\mat I_3\otimes\QQ)\,(N\otimes\mat I_2),
	\qquad
	\mat B_e^{-}
	=
	-(\mat I_3\otimes\QQ)\,\big((N\mat P_e)\otimes\mat I_2\big).
	\label{eq:seg-Be-pm}
\end{equation}
Thus, \(\mat B_e\) first forms the displacement jump between the top and
bottom faces and then rotates it into the local Leg--2 frame.

In block form, each entry being a \(2\times2\) matrix, the jump operator can
be written as
\[
\mat B_e=
\begin{bmatrix}
	N_i(\xi_i)\QQ & N_j(\xi_i)\QQ & N_k(\xi_i)\QQ &
	-\,N_k(\xi_i)\QQ & -\,N_j(\xi_i)\QQ & -\,N_i(\xi_i)\QQ
	\\
	N_i(\xi_j)\QQ & N_j(\xi_j)\QQ & N_k(\xi_j)\QQ &
	-\,N_k(\xi_j)\QQ & -\,N_j(\xi_j)\QQ & -\,N_i(\xi_j)\QQ
	\\
	N_i(\xi_k)\QQ & N_j(\xi_k)\QQ & N_k(\xi_k)\QQ &
	-\,N_k(\xi_k)\QQ & -\,N_j(\xi_k)\QQ & -\,N_i(\xi_k)\QQ
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
In the implementation, the quadratic cohesive segment is evaluated at the
Lobatto nodes, so that \(N=\mat I_3\). In that case the jump operator
reduces to the nodal form
\[
\mat B_e=
\begin{bmatrix}
	\QQ & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & -\QQ\\
	0 & \QQ & 0 & 0 & -\QQ & 0\\
	0 & 0 & \QQ & -\QQ & 0 & 0
\end{bmatrix},
\]
which makes the local openings at the three segment nodes available
directly from the nodal displacement vector.

\paragraph{Line integration, traction map, and element residual.}
Let \(\xi\in[0,1]\) denote the parent coordinate along the segment and
\(l_e\) its physical length. The consistent line-integration matrix for the
quadratic segment is
\begin{equation}
	\mat R
	=
	l_e\int_0^1 N(\xi)^\top N(\xi)\,\mathrm d\xi
	=
	\frac{l_e}{30}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		4 & 2 & -1\\
		2 & 16 & 2\\
		-1 & 2 & 4
	\end{bmatrix}.
	\label{eq:seg-R}
\end{equation}
The cohesive tractions evaluated at the three quadratic stations are stacked
in the local vector
\begin{equation}
	\vb s_e
	=
	\big[
	\sigma_{t,i}\ \sigma_{n,i}\ 
	\sigma_{t,j}\ \sigma_{n,j}\ 
	\sigma_{t,k}\ \sigma_{n,k}
	\big]^\top
	\in\mathbb R^6.
	\label{eq:seg-se}
\end{equation}
These tractions are obtained pointwise from the local openings
\(\bd_e\) through the traction map
\begin{equation}
	\vb s_e=\mathcal T(\bd_e),
	\label{eq:seg-Te}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal T\) is built from the mixed-mode law
Eq.~\eqref{eq:Tcz_mixed}. At each node
\(\ell\in\{i,j,k\}\),
\begin{equation}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_{t,\ell}\\[3pt]
		\sigma_{n,\ell}
	\end{bmatrix}
	=
	\mathbf{T}_{\mathrm{cz}}
	\!\left(
	\bar d_{n,\ell},\bar d_{t,\ell};\,\operatorname{sgn}_0(d_{t,\ell})
	\right),
	\qquad
	\bar d_{t,\ell}=\frac{|d_{t,\ell}|}{d_t^{\max}},
	\qquad
	\bar d_{n,\ell}=\frac{d_{n,\ell}}{d_n^{\max}}.
	\label{eq:seg-node-traction}
\end{equation}

The corresponding nodal resultants on the top face are obtained by line
integration in the local frame followed by rotation to the global frame:
\begin{equation}
	\vb r_e^{+}
	=
	(\mat R\otimes \QQ^\top)\,\vb s_e
	\in\mathbb R^6.
	\label{eq:seg-rplus}
\end{equation}
The bottom-face resultants are equal and opposite, but must be stored in the
reversed node order \((k^-,j^-,i^-)\). Therefore,
\begin{equation}
	\vb r_e^{-}
	=
	-(\mat P_e\otimes \mat I_2)\,\vb r_e^{+}.
	\label{eq:seg-rminus}
\end{equation}
Stacking the two faces gives the cohesive element residual
\begin{equation}
	\vb f_e
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\mat I_6\\
		-(\mat P_e\otimes \mat I_2)
	\end{bmatrix}
	(\mat R\otimes \QQ^\top)\,\vb s_e
	=
	\mat A_e\,\vb s_e
	=
	\mat A_e\,\mathcal T(\bd_e)
	=
	\mat A_e\,\mathcal T(\mat B_e\vb U_e),
	\label{eq:seg-res}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
	\mat A_e
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\mat I_6\\
		-(\mat P_e\otimes \mat I_2)
	\end{bmatrix}
	(\mat R\otimes \QQ^\top)
	\in\mathbb R^{12\times 6}.
	\label{eq:seg-Ae}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:seg-res} makes the element construction explicit:
nodal displacements first generate the local openings through
\(\mat B_e\), these openings are converted into nodal cohesive tractions by
\(\mathcal T\), and the resulting local tractions are then integrated and
assembled into equal-and-opposite face resultants.

\paragraph{Consistent linearization.}
The cohesive element tangent follows directly from the chain rule applied to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:seg-res}:
\begin{equation}
	\mat K_e
	=
	\frac{\partial \vb f_e}{\partial \vb U_e}
	=
	\frac{\partial \vb f_e}{\partial \vb s_e}
	\frac{\partial \vb s_e}{\partial \bd_e}
	\frac{\partial \bd_e}{\partial \vb U_e}
	=
	\mat A_e\,\mat K_{\mathrm{loc},e}\,\mat B_e
	\in\mathbb R^{12\times 12},
	\label{eq:seg-Ke}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
	\mat K_{\mathrm{loc},e}
	=
	\operatorname{blkdiag}
	\!\left(
	\mathbf K_{\mathrm{loc},i},
	\mathbf K_{\mathrm{loc},j},
	\mathbf K_{\mathrm{loc},k}
	\right)
	\in\mathbb R^{6\times 6}.
	\label{eq:seg-Kloc}
\end{equation}
Here \(\mathbf K_{\mathrm{loc},\ell}\) is the \(2\times2\) local tangent at
station \(\ell\), obtained from the Hessian of the mixed-mode potential as
described in Section~\ref{sec:tsl}. Because the constitutive law is
potential-based, each block \(\mathbf K_{\mathrm{loc},\ell}\) is symmetric,
and this symmetry is inherited by the element Jacobian away from the sign
switch at \(d_t=0\).

\paragraph{Global assembly and augmented equilibrium.}
Assembling the element contributions \(\vb f_e\) and \(\mat K_e\) over all
cohesive segments yields the global cohesive residual
\(\vb F_{\mathrm{coh}}(\vb u)\) and the corresponding global tangent
\(\partial\vb F_{\mathrm{coh}}/\partial\vb u\). Let \(\mat K\) denote the
bulk elastic stiffness matrix and \(\vb F_{\mathrm{ext}}\) the unit external
load vector associated with the applied boundary tractions. The unknowns of
the nonlinear problem are the global displacement vector \(\vb u\) and the
scalar load amplitude \(\sigma\).

For a prescribed trial orientation of Leg~2, the critical state is defined by
the condition that the normal opening at the cohesive tip reaches its
critical value \(d_n^{\max}\). The corresponding augmented system is written
as
\begin{equation}
	\vb F_{\mathrm{aug}}(\vb u,\sigma)
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\vb R(\vb u,\sigma)\\[3pt]
		h(\vb u)
	\end{bmatrix}
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\mat K\,\vb u-\sigma\,\vb F_{\mathrm{ext}}-\vb F_{\mathrm{coh}}(\vb u)\\[6pt]
		d_n^{\mathrm{tip}}(\vb u)-d_n^{\max}
	\end{bmatrix}
	=
	\vb 0.
	\label{eq:aug-res}
\end{equation}
Here \(d_n^{\mathrm{tip}}(\vb u)\) is the normal opening of the two tip nodes
of the cohesive continuation, extracted in the local Leg--2 frame.

The Jacobian of the augmented system has the block structure
\begin{equation}
	\mat J_{\mathrm{aug}}
	=
	\frac{\partial \vb F_{\mathrm{aug}}}{\partial(\vb u,\sigma)}
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\mat K-\dfrac{\partial\vb F_{\mathrm{coh}}}{\partial\vb u}
		&
		-\vb F_{\mathrm{ext}}
		\\[10pt]
		\dfrac{\partial h}{\partial\vb u}
		&
		0
	\end{bmatrix}.
	\label{eq:aug-J}
\end{equation}
The row vector \(\partial g/\partial\vb u\) contains only four nonzero
entries, corresponding to the two tip nodes on the upper and lower faces of
Leg~2. If \(\bn\) denotes the local normal of the final cohesive segment,
then
\[
\frac{\partial h}{\partial\vb u}
=
\big[
\cdots\ \bn^\top\ \cdots\ -\bn^\top\ \cdots
\big].
\]

At Newton iteration \(m\), the correction
\((\Delta\vb u,\Delta\sigma)\) is obtained from
\begin{equation}
	\mat J_{\mathrm{aug}}(\vb u^{(m)},\sigma^{(m)})
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\Delta\vb u\\
		\Delta\sigma
	\end{bmatrix}
	=
	-
	\vb F_{\mathrm{aug}}(\vb u^{(m)},\sigma^{(m)}),
	\label{eq:newton-aug}
\end{equation}
followed by the update
\[
\vb u^{(m+1)}=\vb u^{(m)}+\Delta\vb u,
\qquad
\sigma^{(m+1)}=\sigma^{(m)}+\Delta\sigma.
\]
The iterations are stopped once the residual norm and the cohesive-traction
corrections fall below the prescribed tolerances.

This construction separates the interface formulation into three distinct
layers: the kinematic jump operator \(\mat B_e\), the constitutive traction
map \(\mathcal T\), and the consistent local tangent assembled through
\(\mat K_e=\mat A_e\mat K_{\mathrm{loc},e}\mat B_e\). This modular form is
useful both conceptually and computationally: it makes the finite-element
implementation transparent and allows the cohesive law to be modified without
changing the underlying interface assembly. With the discrete interface
operators now defined, the next section turns to the generation of the
channel-based mesh and its collapse to a zero-thickness cohesive interface.

\section{Mesh creation}
\label{sec:mesh}

The mesh must resolve the narrow cohesive segment (Leg~2) while keeping the
global model compact. We generate a thin channel solely to drive triangular
(T3) refinement along the prescribed two-leg path; after meshing, we convert
to quadratic (T6) elements and collapse the channel to obtain a
zero-thickness crack with collocated top/bottom nodes. In the baseline
calculations considered in Sections~\ref{sec:results} and
\ref{sec:LEFM-comparison}, the mesh is constructed for a prescribed trial
cohesive length \(\delta\). Section~\ref{sec:scope_limitations} considers a
separate parametric study in which this auxiliary length is selected
adaptively before meshing.

\paragraph{Channel geometry and face topology (for T3 meshing).}
A narrow channel is built around
\(\Gamma_{\mathrm{cr}}\cup\Gamma_{\mathrm{cz}}\) solely for T3 mesh
generation. Its half-width is tied to the interface resolution,
\[
w_c=\frac{h_{\min}}{16},
\]
chosen empirically to balance refinement enforcement and element quality.
Along Leg~2, the two inner faces are \emph{not merged} during T3 generation;
they are meshed as coincident edges with separate node sets so that the
displacement jump
\[
\bd=\QQ\,\jump{\uu}
\]
is well defined at the interface nodes.

\paragraph{Target sizing and uniform seeding of Leg~2.}
Leg~2 is uniformly seeded into \(n_{\mathrm{coh}}\) stations, which fixes the
near-interface target size
\[
h_{\min}=\frac{\delta}{n_{\mathrm{coh}}}.
\]
The physical crack (Leg~1) is seeded consistently with the adjacent bulk
resolution so that the transition into the cohesive continuation remains
smooth.

\paragraph{Graded size field and far-field cap.}
Away from the channel, the target element size grows geometrically with
factor \(h_{\mathrm{grad}}>1\) up to a far-field cap \(h_{\max}\). This
produces a smooth gradation from fine elements near Leg~2 to coarser
elements in the outer regions, avoids abrupt stiffness changes, and keeps
the system size moderate.

\paragraph{T3\,$\to$\,T6 conversion and channel collapse.}
After T3 generation, all bulk triangles are upgraded to T6. The narrow
channel is then collapsed to the polyline midline so that the two faces
along Leg~2 coincide geometrically while remaining kinematically distinct
through coincident top/bottom node pairs. Interface segments use quadratic
elements with Lobatto nodal evaluation (\(N=\mat I_3\)), matching the
operators of Section~\ref{sec:construction} so that
\[
\bd_e=\mat B_e\vb U_e
\]
and the cohesive tractions are evaluated pointwise at the segment nodes.

\paragraph{Quality and topology checks.}
The refinement band is constructed so as to maintain acceptable aspect
ratios and to avoid sliver elements near the knee \(V_1\). Face orientation
is enforced so that the local frame
\(\QQ=[\bt^\top;\bn^\top]\) points consistently from lower to upper, with
\(\bt\) directed from mouth to tip. Coincident node pairing along Leg~2 is
verified prior to assembly to ensure one-to-one cohesive linkage.

\paragraph{Mesh-convergence criteria.}
Mesh independence is assessed by halving \(h_{\min}\) and, if needed,
adjusting \(h_{\max}\) proportionally while keeping \(h_{\mathrm{grad}}\)
fixed. The critical load \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}\) and kink angle \(\theta_2\)
are deemed converged when the relative changes between successive
refinements fall below \(1\%\). Throughout the construction,
\(w_c=h_{\min}/16\) is used.

\paragraph{Remark on channel collapse versus split-node insertion.}
The present channel-collapse strategy is closely related in spirit to
standard split-node procedures for introducing zero-thickness cohesive
interfaces, but differs in the stage at which mesh resolution is enforced.
In classical split-node approaches, the interface is created \emph{after}
meshing by duplicating nodes and modifying element connectivity along a
prescribed path. While effective for simple, straight cracks, this does not
by itself control the resolution of bulk fields normal to the interface and
often requires additional local remeshing. In contrast, the channel-collapse
approach constructs the mesh \emph{a priori} with a thin refinement band
aligned with the intended cohesive leg, so that the interface
discretization and the surrounding bulk gradation are governed
simultaneously through the size-field parameters
\((h_{\min},h_{\mathrm{grad}},h_{\max})\). The subsequent collapse step
produces a zero-thickness interface with coincident node pairs and ordered
topology without explicit mesh surgery. This entails a modest preprocessing
effort to define the channel geometry, but provides improved robustness,
reproducibility, and resolution control in systematic crack-kinking studies
involving multiple trial paths.

With the interface operators (Section~\ref{sec:construction}) and the
graded, collapsed mesh defined here, the next section reports verification,
mesh convergence, and kink-angle selection under mixed-mode loading. The
sensitivity of the directional prediction to the auxiliary choice of
\(\delta\) is examined separately in
Section~\ref{sec:scope_limitations}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

For each trial direction \(\theta_2\) of Leg~2, we solve the augmented
system to the critical state, defined by the condition that the normal
opening at the mouth of the cohesive continuation reaches the critical
value \(d_n^{\max}\). Newton iterations employ the consistent cohesive
tangent from Section~\ref{sec:tsl}. Interface fields are post-processed in
the local frame \((\bt,\bn)\) attached to Leg~2. Field plots use normalized
variables
\[
\bar\sigma_n=\frac{\sigma_n}{\sigma_n^{\max}},
\qquad
\bar\sigma_t=\frac{\sigma_t}{\sigma_t^{\max}},
\qquad
\bar d_n=\frac{d_n}{d_n^{\max}},
\qquad
\bar d_t=\frac{|d_t|}{d_t^{\max}},
\]
whereas the reported critical load \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}\) remains
dimensional.

All numerical examples use the plate dimensions
\(A=B=10~\mathrm{cm}\) and the physical crack length \(a=2~\mathrm{cm}\).
The angle of the physical crack is \(\theta_1=-20^\circ\). Plane strain is
assumed with \(E=4~\mathrm{GPa}\) and \(\nu=0.3\). The cohesive strengths
are
\[
(\sigma_n^{\max},\sigma_t^{\max})=(40,15.4)~\mathrm{MPa},
\]
obtained from \(\sigma_n^{\max}=E/100\) and
\(\sigma_t^{\max}=G/100\), where \(G=E/[2(1+\nu)]\). The prescribed
fracture energies are
\[
(\phi_n,\phi_t)=(800,1200)~\mathrm{J\,m^{-2}},
\]
with coupling parameter \(r=0.4\). The one-dimensional TSL parameters are
\[
a_1=0.002,
\qquad
a_{2n}=0.9,
\qquad
a_{2t}=0.5.
\]
Using the corresponding values of \(c_\alpha\), the resulting critical
openings are
\[
d_n^{\max}\approx 23.6~\mu\mathrm{m},
\qquad
d_t^{\max}\approx 104~\mu\mathrm{m}.
\]

\subsection{Selection of the kink angle}

\paragraph{Critical state and directional selectors.}
In the present framework, the critical state is defined uniquely and
independently of any directional criterion. For each prescribed trial
orientation of the cohesive continuation, the critical state is reached
when the normal separation at the mouth of the cohesive zone attains its
critical value,
\[
d_n=d_n^{\max},
\]
corresponding to exhaustion of the normal cohesive resistance. The criteria
introduced below do not redefine this failure condition. Instead, they
serve as complementary \emph{selection measures} for identifying the
preferred kink direction, all evaluated at the same critical state.

For the illustrative calculations in this subsection, the directional
response is shown through coarse and fine angular sweeps so that the shape
of the objective function can be inspected directly. In the repeated
parametric study of Section~\ref{sec:scope_limitations}, by contrast, the
minimizing angle is obtained by an automated iterative bracket-and-refine
procedure. The distinction is purely algorithmic: the selector itself is
unchanged, but the automated procedure is more efficient for repeated
calculations.

\paragraph{Load-based selector.}
For a trial direction \(\theta_2\), the critical state is solved and the
corresponding critical load \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}(\theta_2)\) is recorded.
The preferred direction is defined by
\[
\theta_2^{\sigma}
=
\operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\theta_2\in\Theta}
\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}(\theta_2),
\]
where \(\Theta\) denotes the prescribed search set of global angles.
Numerically, the minimum is first localized on a coarse sweep and then
refined on a fine sweep; the reported minimizer is the vertex of a
three-point parabolic interpolation through the discrete minimum and its
two nearest neighbors.

With \((n_{\mathrm{coh}},B/h_{\max})=(40,40)\) and
\(h_{\mathrm{grad}}=1.15\), the coarse sweep yields
\[
\min \sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}=5.65160~\mathrm{MPa}
\quad\text{at}\quad
\theta_2\simeq1.833^\circ.
\]
Refinement to \((n_{\mathrm{coh}},B/h_{\max})=(80,80)\) gives
\[
\min \sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}=5.65151~\mathrm{MPa}
\quad\text{at}\quad
\theta_2\simeq1.727^\circ,
\]
corresponding to an angular shift of \(0.106^\circ\). The corresponding
coarse and refined curves are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:kink-sweeps}, where
diamonds mark the parabolic vertex and dashed lines indicate its position.

\paragraph{Energetic selector.}
As a local alternative, we minimize the mixed-mode cohesive energy stored at
the mouth of Leg~2 in the critical state, normalized by the mode-I
fracture energy:
\begin{equation}
	\theta_2^{\Psi}
	=
	\operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\theta_2\in\Theta}
	\bigl(\hat\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}(\theta_2)-1\bigr),
	\qquad
	\hat\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}=\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}/\phi_n .
\end{equation}
Here \(\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}\) denotes the mixed-mode cohesive potential
evaluated at the mouth at the onset of instability. For the trapezoidal
traction--separation laws used in this work, the normal opening at the
mouth satisfies \(\bar d_n=1\) at the critical state, so that
\[
F(\bar d_n=1)=c_n.
\]
Substituting this relation into the mixed-mode potential yields
\[
\hat\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}
=
1+
\left(
\frac{\phi_t}{\phi_n}\frac{1}{c_t}
-
r\sqrt{\frac{\phi_t}{\phi_n}}
\sqrt{\frac{c_n}{c_t}}
\right)
F(\bar d_t).
\]
Thus, the energetic excess \(\hat\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}-1\) is proportional to
the tangential cohesive potential \(F(\bar d_t)\), and minimizing it is
equivalent to minimizing the shear opening at the mouth.

Applying the same two-stage sweep and parabolic-vertex refinement strategy,
the coarse mesh yields
\[
\theta_2^{\Psi}\simeq3.536^\circ
\]
with
\[
\min(\hat\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}-1)\approx-1.876\times10^{-8}.
\]
The refined sweep gives
\[
\theta_2^{\Psi}\simeq3.586^\circ
\]
with
\[
\min(\hat\Psi_{\mathrm{crit}}-1)\approx-6.131\times10^{-9},
\]
corresponding to an angular shift of \(0.05^\circ\). The near-zero values
confirm numerical consistency of the energetic diagnostic. The
corresponding results are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:kink-psi}.

\paragraph{Conditioning of the load-based selector.}
As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:kink-sweeps}, the function
\(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}(\theta_2)\) exhibits a very shallow minimum in the
vicinity of
\[
\theta_2\approx 1.6^\circ\text{--}1.9^\circ.
\]
Although the minimum can be localized reliably in the present deterministic
setting by parabolic refinement, the pronounced flatness of
\(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}(\theta_2)\) indicates that the load-based criterion is
only weakly conditioned with respect to the kink angle. Consequently, in the
presence of numerical noise, discretization effects, or a more extended
fracture-process zone, small perturbations may lead to noticeable variations
in the estimated minimizing angle.

By contrast, the energetic mouth diagnostic
\(\hat{\Psi}_{\mathrm{crit}}(\theta_2)-1\) exhibits a distinctly steeper and
better-localized minimum. This makes the energetic selector numerically more
robust when the global critical load is only weakly sensitive to the trial
direction and explains why the two selectors play complementary roles in the
present study.

\subsection{Discussion}

The two selectors probe different facets of the critical state and therefore
need not coincide. The load-based criterion is \emph{global}, in the sense
that it reflects equilibrium and the mixed-mode response of the entire
structure at the critical state. In the present calculations, it yields
\[
\theta_2^{\sigma}\approx 1.6^\circ\text{--}1.9^\circ,
\]
and the function \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}(\theta_2)\) exhibits an extremely
shallow minimum in this vicinity. As a result, the load-based selector is
only weakly conditioned with respect to the kink angle, and its minimizer is
sensitive to discretization details and solver tolerances.

By contrast, the energetic mouth criterion is inherently \emph{local}. At the
critical state, the normal opening at the mouth satisfies \(\bar d_n=1\), so
that the normal cohesive response is already on its plateau or early
softening branch. The energetic excess
\(\hat{\Psi}_{\mathrm{crit}}-1\) therefore scales with the tangential
contribution, which remains small and vanishes rapidly. This produces a
steeper and better-localized minimum of
\(\hat{\Psi}_{\mathrm{crit}}(\theta_2)-1\), yielding
\[
\theta_2^{\Psi}\approx 3.586^\circ.
\]
The resulting difference between the two selectors is approximately
\(1.86^\circ\).

In practice, the energetic diagnostic exhibits a pronounced U-shaped profile,
making the parabolic-vertex fit robust even on relatively coarse angular
sweeps. By contrast, the much flatter minimum of
\(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}(\theta_2)\) benefits significantly from refinement and
tight bracketing of the search interval. If a single selector is desired, a
natural compromise would be to minimize the \emph{integrated} cohesive work
along Leg~2 rather than its mouth value alone, or to employ a composite
objective that balances load capacity against cohesive dissipation and
therefore accounts for the response of the full interface.

\paragraph{Remark on cohesive-law regularization and modeling scope.}
The interpretation of the above results is closely tied to the adopted
cohesive regularization. The present framework employs a smooth,
potential-based traction--separation law, with the displacement jump as the
primary kinematic variable and cohesive tractions obtained as its energetic
conjugate. This choice is motivated by numerical robustness and by the
availability of a symmetric algorithmic tangent in mixed-mode problems.

Within this setting, the extent of the \emph{active} cohesive zone is not
prescribed a priori, but emerges implicitly from the solution as the portion
of the interface undergoing separation. Formulations in which the
cohesive-zone length is treated as an explicit unknown correspond to
free-boundary cohesive-crack models and require a different mathematical
setting, which lies beyond the scope of the present finite-element CZM
approach.

\paragraph{Remark on the role of crack and trial-leg lengths.}
Consistent with this modeling scope, both the initial crack and the kinked
segment are introduced here as given geometrical inputs in the
incipient-kinking analysis. The length of the kinked segment should be
regarded as an auxiliary numerical parameter rather than as a physical crack
length. The physically relevant quantity is the \emph{active} cohesive-zone
extent, which emerges from the solution as the portion of the trial interface
that experiences separation. Accordingly, the trial leg is chosen
sufficiently long that the active cohesive zone remains strictly contained
within it; once this consistency condition is satisfied, the predicted kink
direction and critical load are insensitive to the selected trial-leg length.

The estimate \(\ell_p\) serves as a classical scaling guideline to ensure
that the fracture-process zone can fully develop without artificial
truncation; it does not prescribe the cohesive-zone length and is not a
constitutive assumption. Treating the cohesive crack length as a true free
parameter requires a free-boundary or incremental propagation formulation and
therefore lies beyond the scope of the present incipient-kinking study.

\paragraph{Remark on a possible extension to crack propagation.}
While the present study is restricted to the determination of the kinking
direction and associated critical load for a predefined trial leg, the
proposed framework naturally lends itself to extension toward incremental
crack propagation. In such a setting, the crack path would be updated step by
step, and a new cohesive segment would be activated at each increment. The
thin-channel construction and subsequent collapse would then be repeated for
the updated crack geometry, implying regeneration of the mesh only in the
vicinity of the advancing crack.

Although this introduces additional computational cost, the channel-based
meshing procedure is fully automated, local to the crack region, and does not
require explicit mesh surgery or global topological modifications; the
interface operators and nonlinear solution strategy remain unchanged. The
parametric kinking studies reported here indicate that the cost of mesh
generation is comparable to that of the nonlinear solve and scales smoothly
with interface refinement, suggesting that multi-step crack-growth
simulations can be carried out without a prohibitive increase in
computational effort.


\section{Comparison with LEFM predictors}
\label{sec:LEFM-comparison}

This section compares the crack-kinking direction and critical load obtained
from the cohesive-zone framework with classical LEFM-based predictions. The
goal is not to claim exact equivalence between the two descriptions, but to
assess, in the near-LEFM regime considered here, which parts of the response
remain essentially elastic and which retain a measurable imprint of the
finite cohesive zone.

\subsection{Relative angle definition}

Throughout the paper, the kink angle is measured as the signed relative
rotation between the two legs,
\begin{equation}
	\Delta\theta
	=
	\operatorname{wrap}_{(-\pi,\pi]}\!\left(\theta_2-\theta_1\right),
	\label{eq:dtheta_wrap}
\end{equation}
where the wrapping operation ensures that \(\Delta\theta\) is always reported
in the interval \((-\pi,\pi]\), thus preserving the correct sense of
deflection (clockwise or counterclockwise). In plots, angles are reported in
degrees after conversion of the wrapped value.

In the comparison below, we use two decorated forms of the same quantity:
\[
\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{LEFM}}
=
\operatorname{wrap}_{(-\pi,\pi]}\!\left(\theta_2-\theta_1\right),
\qquad
\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{CZM}}
=
\operatorname{wrap}_{(-\pi,\pi]}\!\left(\theta_2-\theta_1\right),
\]
where \(\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{LEFM}}\) is obtained from the maximum-tangential-stress
(MTS) criterion applied to the LEFM stress-intensity factors, and
\(\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{CZM}}\) is obtained from the cohesive critical state.

\subsection{LEFM predictor}

For a given inclination \(\theta_1\) of the physical crack, the LEFM
benchmark is constructed from the mixed-mode stress-intensity factors
\((K_{\mathrm I},K_{\mathrm{II}})\) associated with Leg~1 and the
maximum-tangential-stress criterion. Rather than using closed-form
approximations for inclined edge cracks, all mixed-mode quantities are
computed directly by mode separation of the \(J\)-integral.

\paragraph{Single-contour \(J\)-integral and mirror-point mode decomposition.}
A circular contour of radius \(r_I\) is placed around the crack tip, and the
near-tip fields are sampled at pairs of mirror-symmetric points
\[
\vb P^{+}(\theta)=
\begin{bmatrix}
	r_I\cos\theta\\
	r_I\sin\theta
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\vb P^{-}(\theta)=
\begin{bmatrix}
	r_I\cos\theta\\
	-r_I\sin\theta
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\theta\in(0,\pi),
\]
expressed in local crack-tip coordinates \((x_1,x_2)\), where \(x_1\) is
aligned with the physical crack.

Let \(\bm{\upvarepsilon}_{P^{+}}\), \(\bm{\upsigma}_{P^{+}}\), and
\(\partial\vb u_{P^{+}}/\partial x_1\) denote the strain tensor, stress tensor,
and directional displacement gradient at \(\vb P^{+}(\theta)\). The
corresponding quantities at \(\vb P^{-}(\theta)\) are defined analogously.
Following the mirror-point formulation of Ishikawa, Kitagawa, and Okamura,
the symmetric and antisymmetric parts of the fields are used to extract the
mode-I and mode-II contributions.

For numerical evaluation, the in-plane stress and strain tensors are written
in Voigt form as
\[
\vb\varepsilon=
\begin{bmatrix}
	\varepsilon_{11}\\
	\varepsilon_{22}\\
	\gamma_{12}
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\vb\sigma=
\begin{bmatrix}
	\sigma_{11}\\
	\sigma_{22}\\
	\sigma_{12}
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\gamma_{12}=2\varepsilon_{12}.
\]
The mode-separated strain and stress fields are then obtained as
\begin{align}
	\vb\varepsilon^{\mathrm I}
	&=
	\frac12
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\varepsilon_{P^{+},11}+\varepsilon_{P^{-},11}\\
		\varepsilon_{P^{+},22}+\varepsilon_{P^{-},22}\\
		\gamma_{P^{+},12}-\gamma_{P^{-},12}
	\end{bmatrix},
	&
	\vb\varepsilon^{\mathrm{II}}
	&=
	\frac12
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\varepsilon_{P^{+},11}-\varepsilon_{P^{-},11}\\
		\varepsilon_{P^{+},22}-\varepsilon_{P^{-},22}\\
		\gamma_{P^{+},12}+\gamma_{P^{-},12}
	\end{bmatrix},
	\nonumber\\[4pt]
	\vb\sigma^{\mathrm I}
	&=
	\frac12
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_{P^{+},11}+\sigma_{P^{-},11}\\
		\sigma_{P^{+},22}+\sigma_{P^{-},22}\\
		\sigma_{P^{+},12}-\sigma_{P^{-},12}
	\end{bmatrix},
	&
	\vb\sigma^{\mathrm{II}}
	&=
	\frac12
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_{P^{+},11}-\sigma_{P^{-},11}\\
		\sigma_{P^{+},22}-\sigma_{P^{-},22}\\
		\sigma_{P^{+},12}+\sigma_{P^{-},12}
	\end{bmatrix}.
	\label{eq:mode_sep}
\end{align}
Thus, \(\bm{\upvarepsilon}_{P^{+}}\), \(\bm{\upvarepsilon}_{P^{-}}\),
\(\bm{\upsigma}_{P^{+}}\), and \(\bm{\upsigma}_{P^{-}}\) are second-order tensors in the
continuum description, whereas
\(\vb\varepsilon^{\mathrm I}\), \(\vb\varepsilon^{\mathrm{II}}\),
\(\vb\sigma^{\mathrm I}\), and \(\vb\sigma^{\mathrm{II}}\) denote their
mode-separated in-plane representations in Voigt notation.

The corresponding directional displacement gradients
\(\partial\vb u^{\mathrm I}/\partial x_1\) and
\(\partial\vb u^{\mathrm{II}}/\partial x_1\) are two-component vectors, and
the mode-separated elastic energy densities are
\[
U^{\mathrm I}
=
\frac12\,\vb\sigma^{\mathrm I}\!\cdot\!\vb\varepsilon^{\mathrm I},
\qquad
U^{\mathrm{II}}
=
\frac12\,\vb\sigma^{\mathrm{II}}\!\cdot\!\vb\varepsilon^{\mathrm{II}}.
\]

\paragraph{Mode-separated \(J\)-integrals and stress-intensity factors.}
Let \(\vec{n}=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\) denote the outward normal at
\(P(\theta)\). With
\[
\tvec^{\mathrm I}=\sigma^{\mathrm I}\vec{n},
\qquad
\tvec^{\mathrm{II}}=\sigma^{\mathrm{II}}\vec{n},
\]
the mode-separated \(J\)-integrands projected onto the crack direction
\(x_1\) are
\[
f_{\mathrm I}(\theta)
=
U^{\mathrm I}n_1
-
\tvec^{\mathrm I}\cdot
\frac{\partial\uu^{\mathrm I}}{\partial x_1},
\qquad
f_{\mathrm{II}}(\theta)
=
U^{\mathrm{II}}n_1
-
\tvec^{\mathrm{II}}\cdot
\frac{\partial\uu^{\mathrm{II}}}{\partial x_1}.
\]
Integration over the upper semicircle and doubling for symmetry gives
\begin{equation}
	J_{\mathrm I}
	=
	2r_I\int_0^\pi f_{\mathrm I}(\theta)\,\mathrm d\theta,
	\qquad
	J_{\mathrm{II}}
	=
	2r_I\int_0^\pi f_{\mathrm{II}}(\theta)\,\mathrm d\theta.
	\label{eq:J_modes}
\end{equation}

Using the plane-strain modulus
\[
E'=\frac{E}{1-\nu^2},
\]
the corresponding stress-intensity factors are recovered from the energetic
identities
\begin{equation}
	K_{\mathrm I}=\sqrt{E'J_{\mathrm I}},
	\qquad
	K_{\mathrm{II}}
	=
	\operatorname{sign}(J_{\mathrm{II}})
	\sqrt{E'|J_{\mathrm{II}}|}.
	\label{eq:KIKII}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{LEFM kink-angle predictor.}
The LEFM kink direction is then obtained from the
maximum-tangential-stress criterion,
\begin{equation}
	\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{LEFM}}
	=
	\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{\theta}
	\sigma_{\theta\theta}(K_{\mathrm I},K_{\mathrm{II}},\theta),
	\label{eq:MTS}
\end{equation}
where \(\sigma_{\theta\theta}\) is the classical LEFM circumferential stress
in the crack-tip asymptotic field.

\paragraph{LEFM critical load: quadratic energetic criterion.}
For critical-load predictions, the mode-separated energy-release rates at a
reference load \(\sigma_0\) are
\[
G_{\mathrm I}(\sigma_0)=\frac{K_{\mathrm I}^2}{E'},
\qquad
G_{\mathrm{II}}(\sigma_0)=\frac{K_{\mathrm{II}}^2}{E'}.
\]
Since \(G\propto\sigma^2\) for fixed geometry,
\[
G_{\mathrm I}(\sigma)
=
G_{\mathrm I}(\sigma_0)\left(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma_0}\right)^2,
\qquad
G_{\mathrm{II}}(\sigma)
=
G_{\mathrm{II}}(\sigma_0)\left(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma_0}\right)^2.
\]
Applying the quadratic mixed-mode criterion
\[
\frac{G_{\mathrm I}}{G_{\mathrm Ic}}
+
\frac{G_{\mathrm{II}}}{G_{\mathrm{IIc}}}
=1
\]
yields the LEFM critical load
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}^{(\mathrm{quad})}
	=
	\sqrt{
		\frac{\sigma_0^2}{
			G_{\mathrm I}(\sigma_0)/G_{\mathrm Ic}
			+
			G_{\mathrm{II}}(\sigma_0)/G_{\mathrm{IIc}}
		}
	}.
	\label{eq:sigcr_quad}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{LEFM critical load: Benzeggagh--Kenane criterion.}
As an additional benchmark, we also consider the Benzeggagh--Kenane (BK)
mixed-mode criterion \cite{BenzeggaghKenane1996}, which defines an effective
fracture toughness
\begin{equation}
	G_c(\beta)
	=
	G_{\mathrm Ic}
	+
	\bigl(G_{\mathrm{IIc}}-G_{\mathrm Ic}\bigr)\beta^{\eta},
	\qquad
	\beta
	=
	\frac{G_{\mathrm{II}}}{G_{\mathrm I}+G_{\mathrm{II}}},
	\label{eq:BK}
\end{equation}
where \(\eta>0\) controls the sensitivity of the toughness to mode mixity.
Evaluating the mode-mix parameter \(\beta_0\) at the reference load
\(\sigma_0\), the BK critical load follows from
\(G(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}})=G_c(\beta_0)\):
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}^{(\mathrm{BK})}(\eta)
	=
	\sigma_0
	\sqrt{
		\frac{G_c(\beta_0)}
		{G_{\mathrm I}(\sigma_0)+G_{\mathrm{II}}(\sigma_0)}
	}.
	\label{eq:sigcr_BK}
\end{equation}
Equations~\eqref{eq:sigcr_quad} and~\eqref{eq:sigcr_BK} provide the LEFM
critical-load benchmarks used in the comparison below.

\subsection{LEFM versus CZM mixed-mode fracture predictions}

In the cohesive-zone framework, each loading inclination \(\theta_1\) is
analyzed through a two-stage procedure. For each prescribed trial direction
\(\theta_2\) of the cohesive continuation, a fully nonlinear equilibrium
problem is solved up to the critical state. An outer search over
\(\theta_2\) is then performed, and the preferred direction is selected by
either the global critical load \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}\) or the energetic
mouth functional \(\hat\Psi\). This provides consistent predictions of both
the kink direction and the associated critical load within a unified CZM
setting.

Figure~\ref{fig:LEFM-CZM} compares these predictions with the LEFM
benchmarks. In Fig.~\ref{fig:LEFM-CZM:a}, the relative kink angles
\(\Delta\theta\) are shown for the MTS predictor and for the two cohesive
selectors. In Fig.~\ref{fig:LEFM-CZM:b}, the corresponding critical loads
are compared with the quadratic energetic criterion and with the BK law for
several values of the exponent \(\eta\).

\paragraph{Kink angle.}
For small loading inclinations \(\theta_1\), all approaches yield very close
kink angles, with differences below about \(0.5^\circ\). This confirms that,
in the small-process-zone regime considered here, the cohesive formulation
recovers the expected near-LEFM limit for the directional response. As
\(\theta_1\) increases, systematic differences emerge. The CZM prediction
obtained by minimizing the global critical load \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}\)
produces slightly shallower deflection angles than the LEFM MTS criterion,
with deviations growing gradually and reaching roughly \(1.5^\circ\) at
\(\theta_1=30^\circ\).

By contrast, the energetic selector based on minimizing \(\hat\Psi\) yields
consistently larger deflection magnitudes \(|\Delta\theta|\) than the
load-based selector, with deviations approaching \(3^\circ\) at the largest
inclination considered. This separation reflects the different roles of the
two selectors already observed in Section~\ref{sec:results}: the load-based
criterion is governed by the global variation of the critical load, whereas
the energetic selector gives greater weight to the local opening-dominated
state at the mouth of the cohesive continuation.

\paragraph{Critical load.}
The corresponding critical loads are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:LEFM-CZM:b}. For all loading angles \(\theta_1\), the CZM
predicts lower critical stresses than LEFM, with the difference increasing
as mode-II participation becomes stronger. At \(\theta_1=2^\circ\), the
reduction is below about \(1.5\%\), whereas at \(\theta_1=30^\circ\) it
reaches approximately \(10\%\). The two cohesive selectors produce nearly
identical values of \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cr}}\), with differences remaining
below about \(0.05\%\) over the full range considered. Thus, although the
two selectors may lead to different kink angles, the global load-carrying
capacity is governed primarily by the overall cohesive dissipation rather
than by the local directional diagnostic.

\paragraph{Physical interpretation.}
The parameter \(\theta_1\) controls the mode mixity at the crack tip rather
than the applied load level itself. For small \(\theta_1\), the response is
opening-dominated, and LEFM and CZM therefore yield nearly identical kink
angles and critical loads. As \(\theta_1\) increases, shear participation
modifies the distribution of tractions and separations within the fracture
process zone, even though its overall extent remains governed mainly by the
material parameters and the attained load. This redistribution is captured
naturally by the mixed-mode cohesive law.

The comparison in Fig.~\ref{fig:LEFM-CZM} therefore supports the same
qualitative conclusion reached in the earlier sections: in the near-LEFM
regime, the kink direction remains governed predominantly by the mixed-mode
elastic field, whereas the critical load retains a clearer signature of the
finite cohesive zone and of the traction--separation law. At the same time,
the comparison should not be interpreted as evidence of universal agreement
between LEFM and CZM. The present results apply to a deliberately
small-process-zone setting, in which the cohesive formulation is used as a
regularized finite-process-zone framework for controlled comparison with the
tip-based LEFM predictors.

\section{Sensitivity to the auxiliary cohesive-leg length, range of applicability, and limitations}
\label{sec:scope_limitations}

The critical state in the present formulation remains defined exactly as in
Section~\ref{sec:results}: for each prescribed trial orientation of
Leg~2, the nonlinear problem is solved until the mouth opening reaches the
critical normal separation. What is examined here is a different issue,
namely the influence of the \emph{auxiliary} cohesive-leg length~$\delta$ on
the resulting directional prediction and on the interpretation of the method.
This distinction is important because the present crack-kinking procedure does
not operate at a mathematical crack tip. Instead, it infers the preferred
direction from a finite cohesive continuation that regularizes the singular
elastic field over a short but nonzero process-zone probe.

The present sensitivity study is carried out in a deliberately near-LEFM
regime, taking $\beta/a \approx 0.05$ as a practical benchmark for a small
but finite fracture-process zone. This value should be understood as a
working reference for controlled comparison, not as a universal criterion for
the validity of LEFM.

\subsection{Calibrated adaptive estimation of the auxiliary cohesive length and its numerical robustness}
\label{subsec:adaptive_delta}

The present crack-kinking procedure does not evaluate a tip criterion at a
mathematical crack tip. Instead, it infers the preferred propagation direction
from a short cohesive continuation of finite length~$\delta$, which
regularizes the singular elastic field over a finite process-zone probe. For
this reason, the choice of~$\delta$ is not a secondary implementation detail:
it must be large enough to contain the full active cohesive zone, but it
should not vary in an uncontrolled way when material parameters are changed.

In the present formulation, $\delta$ is not treated as a material parameter.
It is an auxiliary numerical length introduced to represent the finite
fracture-process zone in a controlled way. If $\delta$ were kept fixed while
the cohesive strength varied, the activated part of the cohesive segment could
occupy very different fractions of the trial leg. In that case, changes in the
predicted kink direction would reflect not only the constitutive variation
itself, but also an inconsistent relation between the finite cohesive zone and
the imposed probe geometry. To avoid this difficulty, the present calculations
use a calibrated adaptive estimate of~$\delta$.

We introduce the stiffness-to-strength ratio
\begin{equation}
	\rho=\frac{E}{\sigma_n^{\max}},
\end{equation}
and define the effective stress-intensity measure
\begin{equation}
	K_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sqrt{K_I^2+K_{II}^2}.
\end{equation}
A Dugdale-type estimate is then used for the characteristic process-zone size,
\begin{equation}
	r_D=C_D\left(\frac{K_{\mathrm{eff}}}{\sigma_n^{\max}}\right)^2,
\end{equation}
where $C_D$ is a dimensionless calibration constant. The trial cohesive length
is chosen from
\begin{equation}
	\delta=\frac{r_D}{r_\beta},
\end{equation}
where $r_\beta$ is the target fraction of the active cohesive length within
the trial segment, and $\beta$ denotes the activated part of Leg~2 in the
converged critical state.

Here $C_D$ is not taken as universal. Rather, it is calibrated separately for
each $\rho$-sweep so that the activated cohesive zone occupies approximately
the same fraction of the trial segment throughout that sweep. For the three
initial crack inclinations considered here, the adopted values are
$C_D=17.2$ for $\theta_1=-30^\circ$,
$C_D=22.36=1.3\times 17.2$ for $\theta_1=-40^\circ$, and
$C_D=34.4=2.0\times 17.2$ for $\theta_1=-50^\circ$. Accordingly, the present
estimate of~$\delta$ should be interpreted as a calibrated predictor rather
than as a universal closed-form law.

This gives the procedure a predictor--corrector character. The LEFM field
provides a first estimate of the process-zone scale through $K_{\mathrm{eff}}$,
while the subsequent CZM solution checks a posteriori whether the activated
length~$\beta$ indeed occupies the intended fraction of the trial cohesive
segment. The role of the study below is therefore not merely parametric. It is
to verify that this normalization remains effective as the stiffness-to-strength
ratio changes.

A further algorithmic distinction should be emphasized. In Section~7, the kink
angle is presented through coarse and fine angular sweeps in order to
illustrate directly the shallow minimum of $\sigma_{cr}(\theta_2)$ and the
sharper minimum of the energetic selector. In the present parametric study,
however, the selected angle $\theta_2^\ast$ is determined by an automated
iterative procedure that repeatedly brackets and refines the minimizing angle
until the prescribed angular tolerance is reached. This replacement is purely
algorithmic: it does not alter the directional criterion itself, but makes the
determination of $\theta_2^\ast$ fully automatic and computationally efficient
for repeated calculations. In this sense, the procedure is already suitable as
the directional core of a future stepwise crack-growth algorithm, although the
corresponding propagation study lies beyond the scope of the present paper.

Table~\ref{tab:rho_sweep_all} summarizes the results obtained on the finer mesh
used for production calculations, with
\[
n_{\mathrm{coh}}=80,\qquad B/h_{\max}=80,\qquad h_{\mathrm{grad}}=1.15.

$r_D\propto K_{\mathrm{eff}}^2/(\sigma_n^{\max})^2$: with fixed $E$,
increasing $\rho$ corresponds to decreasing cohesive strength and therefore to
a larger predicted process-zone size. Second, the ratio $\beta/\delta$ remains
nearly constant throughout each sweep. This confirms that the calibrated
LEFM-based estimate of~$\delta$ regularizes the finite cohesive problem in a
quantitatively controlled manner. Third, the selected kink angle
$\theta_2^\ast$ varies only weakly with~$\rho$ within each sweep. Thus, once
the process zone is represented consistently, moderate changes in the
stiffness-to-strength ratio primarily rescale the finite cohesive length while
affecting the directional prediction only slightly.

\subsection{Relation to LEFM, applicability, and potential failure modes}
\label{subsec:scope_failure_modes}

The present results should be interpreted relative to LEFM with care. In
classical LEFM, the propagation criterion is evaluated at a mathematical crack
tip and depends only on asymptotic quantities such as $K_I$ and $K_{II}$. In
the present CZM framework, by contrast, the propagation direction is inferred
from a finite cohesive segment carrying distributed tractions. For this
reason, exact agreement with LEFM criteria is not expected in general, even
when the trends are similar. The two descriptions coincide only in an
asymptotic regime in which the process zone becomes vanishingly small
compared with all geometric scales. That limit is not established here.
Accordingly, the present method should be viewed as a finite-process-zone
formulation whose relation to LEFM is asymptotic rather than exact.

Within that interpretation, the method is expected to be most reliable for
quasi-static mixed-mode crack growth in quasi-brittle solids when the
fracture-process zone remains small compared with the ligament size and the
other structural dimensions. Outside this regime, several failure modes should
be anticipated. If the traction--separation law departs strongly from the
present class of laws, for example through pronounced tension--shear
asymmetry, strong rate dependence, or markedly different softening structure,
the Dugdale-type predictor for $\delta$ may cease to keep $\beta/\delta$
approximately constant and may require a different calibration or an
additional adaptive loop. If the process zone becomes comparable with the
structural dimensions, the local interpretation of the cohesive segment as a
short directional probe loses validity, and the predicted direction may then
reflect the imposed probe geometry as much as the underlying fracture
physics. If the interface discretization is insufficient, both the activated
length~$\beta$ and the directional diagnostic itself may be distorted. The
adaptive estimate of $\delta$ mitigates this difficulty but does not remove
the need for mesh verification. Finally, the present formulation does not
include inertia, large-scale plasticity, or fully developed viscoelastic
fracture; dynamic fracture, highly ductile response, and hereditary
constitutive effects therefore lie outside the validated scope of the present
study.

Taken together, these observations define the meaning of the present
validation more precisely. The results do not establish universal agreement
with LEFM, nor do they show parameter-independence of the CZM direction in a
general sense. They show, rather, that within the targeted quasi-static
quasi-brittle regime, the predictor--corrector choice of the auxiliary
cohesive length provides a consistent and numerically robust basis for
comparing directional predictions across a family of cohesive parameters.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This work developed and analyzed a fully implicit cohesive-zone framework
for mixed-mode crack kinking. The formulation augments a pre-existing crack
by a short, zero-thickness cohesive continuation, which makes it possible to
evaluate the critical state associated with any prescribed trial kink
orientation in a systematic and well-posed way. A potential-based mixed-mode
traction--separation law is combined with a channel-collapse meshing
strategy, so that the cohesive response is energetically consistent, the
local tangent is symmetric, and the interface can be resolved without
enrichment techniques or explicit mesh surgery.

The study was carried out deliberately in a near-LEFM regime, so that the
relationship between cohesive and elastic predictions could be assessed in a
controlled way. Within this setting, three main conclusions emerge.

First, the kink direction remains governed predominantly by the mixed-mode
elastic field. Across the loading configurations considered here, the
cohesive predictions of the deflection angle remain close to the classical
LEFM maximum-tangential-stress predictor. This does not represent a new
physical crack-deflection mechanism; rather, it shows that the present
cohesive formulation recovers the expected near-LEFM directional behavior in
a numerically robust and energetically consistent manner.

Second, the critical load retains a clearer signature of the finite
fracture-process zone and of the traction--separation law. Although LEFM and
CZM both predict the same qualitative trends with increasing mode mixity,
the cohesive model consistently yields lower critical loads, with the
difference becoming more pronounced as the mode-II contribution grows. In
this sense, the finite process zone has a much stronger influence on the
load level than on the selected kink direction.

Third, the numerical framework is robust provided the interface and bulk
discretizations are chosen consistently. The convergence studies show that
accurate and mesh-independent critical loads are obtained once the
cohesive-leg resolution and far-field truncation are sufficiently refined,
and the reconstructed bulk tractions agree closely with the cohesive
tractions along almost the entire interface. Among the two directional
selectors considered here, the load-based minimum is shallow and therefore
only weakly conditioned, whereas the energetic mouth criterion exhibits a
sharper minimum and is numerically more stable.

A further conclusion of the present work concerns the role of the auxiliary
cohesive-leg length. The sensitivity study shows that this length should not
be interpreted as a material parameter, but as a numerical regularization
scale that must be chosen so that the active cohesive zone remains well
contained within the trial segment. Within the quasi-static, small-process-zone
regime considered here, a calibrated predictor--corrector choice of the
auxiliary length provides a consistent basis for comparing directional
predictions across a family of cohesive parameters. At the same time, this
also clarifies the scope of the method: the present formulation is intended
for controlled comparison with LEFM in quasi-brittle mixed-mode settings,
not as a general interface law for arbitrary large-separation paths, highly
ductile response, or strongly rate-dependent fracture.

Overall, the principal contribution of this study lies not in proposing a
new fracture criterion, but in establishing a cohesive-zone framework that
is energetically consistent, numerically transparent, and suitable for
assessing the robustness of classical LEFM kinking predictions in the
presence of a finite but small fracture-process zone. The same ingredients
also provide a natural foundation for future work on incremental
crack-path computation, in which the cohesive continuation would be updated
step by step as the crack advances.

\paragraph{Outlook.}
Two extensions appear particularly natural. The first is a broader
sensitivity study with respect to the detailed shape of the
traction--separation law, including the strengthening interval, plateau, and
softening branch. The second is extension to incremental crack growth, for
which the present automated angle-selection procedure and channel-based mesh
construction already provide the essential directional and geometrical
building blocks.
